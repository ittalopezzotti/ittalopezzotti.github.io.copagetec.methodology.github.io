1.    METHODOLOGY APPLIED

The methodology used to achieve the operation, construction and start-up of CACOTE, which consists of the development and operation of a PID temperature controller in a closed space for testing low-cost sensors, was the following:

 

A proposal for a thermal system to test sensors, devices, materials and biological elements was studied, a list of requirements was made, then the physical and chemical phenomena were determined, the conceptual idea was developed, the composition of the system and some elements such as actuators and sensors, followed by a possible presentation of the system and some accessories, part of the electrical system was tested such as voltages and currents in the actuators, some important temperature and humidity sensors were reviewed, then the theoretical model was made, the thermal model was passed, the system was tested, it was mathematically modeled and then tests and validations were carried out.

3.    DETERMINATION OF PHYSICAL PHENOMENA

Physical phenomenon and conceptual idea of ​​the system, in reality the phenomenon is based on temperature with volume, constant pressure, where the equation of the law of gases can be used, for this the need of a system that allows generating temperature ramps from room temperature to about 120ºC of temperature is defined. Some requirements are that the volume and pressure are constant and that the system is closed to allow measuring sensors, devices, biological material and their responses in temperature with conditions of constant pressure and volume and thus find the mass (only applies to biological materials). On the other hand, the system must allow going from room temperature to -10 ºC to test other materials or liquids and see if fractures are created. The system must be able to go from high to low temperature, and from low to high, also allowing stability at a temperature over time. In addition to all this, applications such as incubation periods for biological material, temperature ramps, temperature inversion and stability for periods, creation of controlled disturbances, maximum and minimum values, all these conditions, must be considered. Other aspects also come into play, such as costs and the public to which the development is directed.

4.    PRESENTATION OF THE SYSTEM AND ACCESSORIES

In this work, a system composed of several sensors and actuators is presented, which allow generating and controlling the temperature conditions inside a container. The objective is to obtain stable thermal cycles (temperature versus time curves) so that it is possible to test conformance of sensor temperature ranges in respect to their data sheets, before performing an experiment. The system was designed to obtain data of real conditions within closed spaces, and then compare them with results obtained in larger spaces and thus establish their proportionality relationship.

8.    THERMAL MODEL DEVELOPMENT

The developed system is based on a closed container with a transient air flow, in order to determine at certain times in a confined and known space, a thermal condition that allows us to make measurements under controlled and stable conditions, however the thermodynamic laws of energy and matter exchange are applied since the system takes and exchanges these conditions frequently. In this case there are exchanges of energy between hot and cold objects or air, vapor and oxygen molecules.

 

Figure 7  Container for thermodynamic balance

 

There are three types of systems in thermodynamics: open, closed and isolated.

a.    When this container works with the lid open, it can be defined as an open system that exchanges energy (when the resistors Ra, Rb, Rc are heated) and matter (in this case, air containing oxygen and water molecules is used), all of this with its environment (in our case, the laboratory).

b.    When this container system has its lid closed, it behaves like a closed system (theoretically), and on the contrary, it can only exchange energy with its surroundings (i.e. with the container walls, resistors, fans and contents, sensors), but this system does not materialize in real conditions. The pressure opens the lid and the temperature after 121 ºC deforms the lid and the container, heating the entire surface, so in theory it would approach a closed system.

c.    It could also be used as an isolated system, which is one that cannot exchange matter or energy with its surroundings, which could theoretically work. However, a perfect isolated system is unlikely, but the behavior of such a system is like a thermos cup with a lid and could be conceptually similar to a true isolated system. The elements inside can exchange energy with each other, which explains why drinks get colder and ice melts a little, but they exchange very little energy (heat) with the outside environment.

Figure 8  Closed system with transient air and energy system.

 

In summary, our system is a closed system with a transient flow of air and energy, managed by solenoid valves that open and close the system, to exchange energy and water vapor.

 

For the initial state, the constant volume of the container is considered as known, the medium will be air because variables such as density, temperature and humidity are equal inside and outside the chamber in the first interval from room temperature to 60 °C. The 5 watts electric heating element (Ra) is the actuator in this temperature zone, then the 150-watt electric heating element to heat the systems up to 200°C and finally the cooling effect (Rc). 10. TESTING AND VALIDATION

·         The set of data obtained experimentally in this work, can serve as a benchmark in optimization processes of closed loop control algorithms in closed chamber thermal application systems, which includes a wide variety of thermal processes.

·         The three mathematical models can be used in a wide range of applications, even in larger volumes but preserving the same geometry, because in the system includes the losses by enclosures, infiltrations of air, and losses by isolation; this allows to keep the system stable, scalable, easy installation and affordable.

·         The calibration curves of this work can be compared with systems that have the same thermal behavior, the same geometry and volume scalability. When comparing the data of this experiment with those of a new system that is built with actuators and sensors of similar characteristics, it will be possible to establish relationships between the proposed model and the new data, according to the measurement scale.

·         Additionally, the first order PID control algorithm applied in the thermal systems for each of the actuators includes technical applications in dehydration, cooling, incubation and thermal cycles in ovens.

·         The electronics and hardware is open source as well as the software for processing the digital control signals and the delayed times processed to optimize the output of the actuators during the heating and cooling cycles.

11. PID CONTROL ALGORITHMS

In order to observe the dynamic behavior and inertia of the system, data was captured in different scenarios. The first scenario consisted in heating the air with the low-power resistance (5 watts). The second in heating the air with the high-power resistance (150 watts). The third was to cool naturally by disconnecting the heaters until reaching ambient temperature (the slope of the temperature versus time curve is moderate and governed by the heat losses through the walls of the container). Finally, the fourth scenario consisted of the cooling process through the Peltier modules. It can inferred that the internal fan was able to homogenize the internal temperature by mixing the hot air around the heaters.
